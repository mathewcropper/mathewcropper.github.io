Perception is an interesting thing. Your career is a constant tension between evidence and the perception of both it and you. Your success is an outcome of how well you manage both.

## Merit

On paper, merit is the easiest factor to understand in this whole equation. You either did the thing, or you didn't. You either meet the criteria, or you don't. You either did it better than someone else, or you didn't.

When you start out in your career, and invariably throughout it, merit is the metric by which you naturally measure yourself. You put in the effort, you work the hours, you steadily deliver bigger and better things. Merit is your yardstick, and you think you're doing pretty well.

Deep down, though, you know that this isn't quite enough. Something is amiss.

When preparing this post I spoke with [Taheera](https://twitter.com/taheerah_atchia), an IT consultant, about merit and she nailed it:

> I do fundamentally believe in meritocracy - that if you do good work then you should be rewarded for it. Unfortunately, you can't expect everyone to just automatically see when you've done a good job.
>
> <cite>Taheerah Atchia, interviewed 21 Nov 2016</cite>

That's the rub. In your working life you generally aren't the person who holds the yardstick.

Someone else is the one doing the measuring, and they are, in many ways, the gatekeepers for your career progression. If you want to get on, the people with a seat at the decision-making table need to share and buy in to your merit story.

Perception is at the heart of this. Unlike merit, this is the most complicated factor in the equation, and undoubtedly the most problematic.

## What's perception, anyway?

Good question. Almost everyone I asked said something along the lines of **"It's what people think of you"**, and at its most simplistic, they're right, of course. It is.

In reality, it's an awful lot more nuanced than that. Take the recent US election, ending with a Donald Trump win. A nation divided. Millions of people with very, very different perceptions of one man. Man of the people, biggot, or something else?

The truth is, we apply our own biases, prejudices, and experiences to the things we choose to believe about a person. We use these to fill in the gaps in our knowledge, and these assumptions, whether made consciously or not, affect the way we react to and treat that person. They make us more likely to treat someone more favourably if their behaviours align with our own prejudices, for example.

You may not realise you're doing it, but trust me.. we are. [Confirmation bias](https://www.sciencedaily.com/terms/confirmation_bias.htm) is all around us. We do it every day.

Your success is, therefore, a factor of the following:

* The things you have done
* The things I happen to know you have done
* The things I happen to know about you
* The things I have done and believe
* The things others say about you
* The prejudices I may not consciously realise I have
* A value judgement based on all of the above

Not a lot in there is all that objective, eh? That's perception in a nutshell.

> Half the problem is you, and half the problem is other people.
>
> <cite>Elspeth Kirkman, interviewed 25 Nov 2016</cite>
