title: "Musings on 'Continuous Improvement' and process"
date: 2014-06-03
---

I hate the phrase ‘Continuous Improvement’. It conjures in my mind the image of innovation days, innovation champions, written feedback, suggestion boxes. It makes me think of all of the organisations I’ve worked with who see that they are stagnating but fail to grasp the basic concept behind their stagnation: the desire to adapt and change is a state of mind, not a question of process rigour.

Go read [the wikipedia entry on Kaizen](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaizen) and come back if you don’t know the term. That’s what I’m talking about. Everyone feeling a sense of ownership, everyone empowered to speak up to suggest improvements (big or small). If you are relying on enforced ‘innovation days’ then I fear the boat has sailed for you. Stop doing that, and think about it a little differently. Here are my suggestions for how you could go about making a big change with a slow burn (nb. slow burn = high buy in):

- **CULTURE:** You have to build a culture of people recognising and suggesting process improvements as part of their day to day job. It’s not an occasional focus, or the ‘side of the desk’ responsibility of that dude over there.
- **MECHANISMS:** There are all kinds of ways you can do that, but I’d suggest as a starter for ten implementing agile delivery style practices like regular ideation/retrospective sessions and daily stand ups, and then seeing where you need to go next. If you are starting with a team that’s new to this way of thinking, then you should probably take the time to structure your ideation/retro sessions thematically at first. As the team matures you can move into a more team-driven culture of deciding what the conversations need to be. For the first few sessions use a strong facilitator.
- **PROCESS:** I hate to admit it, but this kind of thing does need just a little bit of process to support it. If people are going to believe in this and make subtle changes on a journey to their own empowerment they need to know that their contributions are regularly solicited, discussed with equity, and acted upon as agreed. This requires a little leadership and ownership, particularly in teams separated across multiple locations.

Continuous improvement isn’t a process you decide upon and implement, it’s a seed that you plant in your business and then let it grow. You should view this as a cultural journey, not a business process change.
