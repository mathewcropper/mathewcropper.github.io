## <a name="mental-models"></a>Mental models and behavioural insights

So, you're building a product and it's evolving. You're guiding its evolution to help your users and your business achieve a bunch of things. You're going to change the world. We all are.

Mental models help me make sense of why I'm doing things the way I am. [Wikipedia the shit out of it](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_model). It's a good wiki, and worth a read. In a nutshell, a mental model is an explanation of a thought process. Referring to one helps me make sense of things, and often encourages me to look at things in different ways.

Behavioural insights are similar, but different. They're also the why, but more about the subconscious drivers that make us do things. I find these particularly useful when I'm thinking about my users' evolving experience and behaviours.

Finding the time to understand research in both of these areas, and then finding even more time to apply it to your work, will help you build products that are far more nuanced and well considered than most.

Here are a bunch of great references for you if this takes your interest:

* [Mental Models I Find Repeatedly Useful](https://medium.com/@yegg/mental-models-i-find-repeatedly-useful-936f1cc405d#.2h0bknt3u)
* [The behaviour wizard](http://www.behaviorwizard.org/wp/)
* [Cognitive lode](http://coglode.com)
* [The Behavioural Insights Team blog](http://www.behaviouralinsights.co.uk/blog/)

## <a name="feedback"></a>Feedback and 'radical candour'

"Man, you're the best!" or "Man, you're the worst!" are both bits of feedback, they tell me how I'm doing, but not much more than that. Emotive feedback is easy, and useful feedback is hard. 

![](https://media.giphy.com/media/1iTpx5PpzRugcrZK/giphy.gif)

We've all got it wrong before, whether we knew it or not. We've all received feedback we don't like, as well.

Thinking about what makes great feedback is important, and here are some good reads, from different points of view; how we break down failure in a team, how we deal with negative feedback from our customers, and how being direct with one another can make feedback more useful.

## <a name="xcode-for-mockups"></a>Using Xcode for mockups

Apps like Omnigraffle or Sketch are awesome tools for building design mockups. They pretty quick, and you can get hold of stencils to be even quicker. They're part of your bread and butter toolkit if you're a product manager responsible for software with a visual element to it.

There is something to be said for building semi-functional mockups, though. I had great success in recruiting customers to use the [Zendesk Support SDK](https://www.zendesk.com/embeddables/) by building quick, working mockups of their apps with it installed. On top of that, it's easier to communicate ideas for how things could/should work visually by knocking it together in a development environment.

Thankfully, Apple's Xcode is very visual, and you can build an app prototype quickly using drag and drop techniques. Of course, it won't _do_ anything, but you'll have something people can touch and interact with. If you're confident with a little Objective-C or Swift, you can really make it sing.

Here are a couple of great posts on the topic of using Xcode for prototypes or mockups.

* [Build an iOS App. From Sketch to the App Store.](https://designcode.io/xcode)
* [Xcode as a Prototyping Tool for Designers](https://robots.thoughtbot.com/xcode-as-a-prototyping-tool-for-designers)

## <a name="feature-fixation"></a>Feature fixation - Tweetbot and regex

For some strange reason, I've thought a lot about regular expressions lately. I've even played [a game](http://regex.alf.nu) ([or two](https://regexcrossword.com/challenges/hexagonal/puzzles/5)). As a result, my feature fixation for this post is [Tweetbot 4 for iOS](https://itunes.apple.com/us/app/tweetbot-4-for-twitter/id1018355599?mt=8) and its regular expression mute filters.

It's reasonable to say that regex would be an advanced feature of any consumer product like this one. That's why I **love** their implementation. First up, here's how you get to it:

* Open up Tweetbot
* Navigate to the mute filters section
* Tap on 'add a keyword'
* Type a regular expression in the text field

You'll see the UI change, showing a new toggle option for regular expression. It's this change that I love. Why? Having it hidden means that your average user won't be confused by the option, and then showing it only when it looks like someone is writing a regular expression is a lovely touch.

It's also pretty interesting from a technical point of view. That's some nice on-the-fly text validation going on here.
