We build SDKs that allow our customers to embed support features (like ticketing, live chat, knowledgebases) in their native apps. We're on a journey with those SDKs to make as much open source as we can, and we started by [making our Unity 3D plugin open source](https://developer.zendesk.com/blog/if-you-build-it-they-will-come).

It was obvious very early on that we would be far more successful in making our SDKs open source if the community around them was active.

Thus, the #zendesk-sdk Slack channel was born. The idea was to start it small and super engaged, and then grow it, until eventually we could make it a public channel.

It's been around for a little while, and so I thought it was time to share what we learned.

## Our starting position

The idea was simple. For the experiment, we'd get our most engaged customers using the SDK, or planning to, and we would invite them to the channel.

All of the people we invited were in either the gaming or lifestyle apps industries, and all of them were people that I engaged with at least a few times a week.

There was a mix of experience; some support managers, some developers, some project managers.. but all interested in and very highly engaged when it comes to our SDK products.

One thing they all had in common was that they'd usually reach out to me if they wanted to share or learn something, rather than creating a ticket or using [our community forums](https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/community/topics/200488257-Zendesk-SDKs). They seemed to be the ideal candidates for a more real time community.

## The experience

From the very beginning, we wanted the channel to feel special. Everyone received the invite below, welcoming them to the channel. We also asked them to introduce themselves when joining the channel.

![](/images/posts/20160612_welcome.png)

I set upon the following ground rules for myself, trying to kindle the community fire:

* Facilitate introductions whenever I can
* All product announcements will happen in the Slack channel first
* Once a week, share what we're working on or give a sneak peak into new features
* Be quick to respond if someone asks a question and the wider group fails to answer

Beyond this, I wanted to see what happened when enthusiastic people were in the same place for long enough. Would the community blossom?

## Lessons learned

Oh my, I learned a lot of lessons.

The Slack channel hasn't yet become the community I hoped it would. I remain hopeful, as it's early days still. It has become something akin to an announcements feed, and the members, although very enthusiastic and interested, didn't really get into an *asking and sharing* mode.

Here are my thoughts one why..

### Some stuff will always be private

Encouraging people to talk about the SDKs is easy. Getting them to talk about what they're doing, or planning to do, with it is hard.

I know for a fact that every one of the members of that channel are doing some seriously cool shit with our SDKs, but they don't want to share it. We anticipated there would be a degree of this, but I was surprised how much of an impact that would have.

> A few of us work in the same industry. Why would I give away to them that cool feature we're working on?

My guess is that when these features are released, people will share more more about them. At the very least, they'll announce them.

Maybe we are in danger of the channel becoming just an announcements feed. If that's the kind of community we have, then the best will in the world won't change that.

### Old ways die hard

Everyone has my Skype and mobile number already, and they're used to using it. After the Slack group came into existence, that didn't change.

My plan is to ask to move certain, more general conversations over to the Slack channel. Over time, my hope is that the Slack channel becomes a more natural starting point.

### We're not your full-time concern

The nature of our SDKs is such that once we're implemented, you can pretty much forget about us. We aren't your full time concern, and so that means that members of the community will end up being ephemeral in nature. We should expect peaks and troughs of engagement and interest.

### Actively grooming

There were two days where I was in back to back workshops, and so wasn't actively participating in the channel. At the end of the second day I got this iMessage:

![](/images/posts/20160612_yousuck.png)

**Oops.** I felt bad, apologised, and got to replying to what I'd missed. It really did bring home how actively the channel needs to be tended, though.

For now, it's entirely manageable. I pay attention to it throughout the day, and when I'm busy, our tech lead takes over. There's always somebody watching.

It is a good lesson for the future, though. I have to ask myself, is this in danger of becoming a support channel, rather than a community? What would the expectations of us be if we made it completely open?

### Threading

This kind of thing just doesn't work without threading. I guess it's a common problem for users of Slack, but overlapping conversations are incredibly tedious to pick apart.

Threading is something that Slack [are planning to release this year](http://www.theverge.com/2016/4/13/11418594/slack-threaded-messaging-chat-app-feature), and I'd say it's a must-have if you're planning to use a channel for something similar to us.

## Success or failure?

In spite of the lessons we learned, I believe this was a success. We have no plans to ditch the channel, and we'll continue to actively bring people in to join us.

Our customers seem to love that we did this, even if it isn't (yet) the wildly active and flourishing place I dreamed of. From a relationship point of view, the channel was an overwhelming success.

I also learned a lot about our particular community, and about live channels like this. Hopefully, these learnings will encourage others to give it a try, but perhaps with more realistic early expectations than I had ;)
