> This post contains spoilers about an event that will take place in 2368. It was shown in the episode *Silicon Avatar* of ST:TNG, which aired in 1991, so hopefully you won’t mind.

You can find a potted history of the Crystalline Entity [here](http://en.memory-alpha.org/wiki/Crystalline_Entity). It’s basically an enormous crystal-like being that floats in space, devouring living things from planets as nourishment. It appeared a couple of times in Star Trek history, but was [destroyed in 2368 by the Enterprise](http://en.memory-alpha.org/wiki/Silicon_Avatar_(episode)). They didn’t want to, though. So, what happened?

Picard supposed that the entity was a living thing and wanted to try to communicate with it, rather than destroy it. A rather strange xenologist by the name of Marr, who’s son was killed by the entity, wasn’t too keen on that idea and so deliberately sabotaged the programme she wrote such that it would kill the entity instead. It did.

Now, a quick summary of what [pair programming](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_programming) is. Instead of having one person build a piece of code, you have two people work on it at the same time; you agree an approach, and then one drives whilst the other observes. This way of programming is great because it means that poor design decisions get spotted quicker, code quality is improved, and everyone is happy because there are fewer defects and unintended side-effects.

If Picard had insisted on this most excellent way of working, Marr’s dodgy code and security overrides would have been easily spotted by even the most disengaged ensign. The programme designed to communicate with the Crystalline Entity would have done its intended job; the entity would have been saved from a cruel fate (vibrated to death!) and they could have potentially found a way to sustain it without it having to kill everything in sight.

So, next time you’re doing that sensitive, mission critical piece of work, just spare a thought for the Crystalline Entity and consider whether pair programming is right for you.
